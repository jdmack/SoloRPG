# Resolution System

## Purpose

This file defines how the GM resolves uncertainty during play.

The Resolution System exists to make outcomes consistent, transparent, and reproducible across independent GM instances.

This file is engine documentation. It must remain campaign-agnostic.

---

# 1. Core Principle

Reality exists before rolls.

Rolls discover reality. Rolls do not create reality.

The GM must determine what is true in the world before using mechanics to discover what the player character perceives, understands, accomplishes, risks, or changes.

The GM must not use rolls to decide whether established reality exists.

Rolls may determine:

- Quality.
- Speed.
- Risk.
- Completeness.
- Cost.
- Exposure.
- Precision.
- Consequence severity.

Rolls should not generally determine whether critical facts, clues, or world elements exist.

---

# 2. When To Roll

The GM should roll when all of the following are true:

- The outcome is uncertain.
- The outcome matters.
- Character capability is relevant.
- There is a meaningful consequence for different result levels.

Common roll situations include:

- Recon.
- Investigation.
- Social pressure.
- Deception detection.
- Stealth.
- Technical work.
- Physical risk.
- Combat.
- Time-sensitive action.
- Avoiding exposure.
- Interpreting ambiguous evidence.

The GM should not roll when:

- Success is obvious.
- Failure is obvious.
- The task is trivial.
- The outcome has no meaningful consequence.
- The player is only asking for directly observable information.
- The roll would bypass a meaningful decision point.
- The roll would decide whether a critical clue exists.

---

# 3. Resolution Inputs

The GM resolves uncertainty using:

- Roll.
- Stat.
- Skill.
- Modifier.
- Difficulty.

The GM should select the stat and skill based on the player's declared approach.

The GM should not force one mechanical interpretation if the player has described a different plausible approach.

The GM should consider:

- Character capability.
- Fictional positioning.
- Preparation.
- Tools.
- Time available.
- Risk level.
- Environmental conditions.
- Opposition.
- Prior information.
- Relationship state.
- Reputation state.

---

# 4. Character Capability

Character sheets are authoritative.

The GM must actively reference character capabilities when resolving uncertainty.

Character capability may affect:

- Whether a roll is needed.
- Which skill applies.
- Difficulty.
- Modifier.
- Quality of information.
- Speed of completion.
- Risk of complication.
- Completeness of result.
- What professional considerations are surfaced.

The GM may use established expertise to give the character useful observations or likely considerations without a roll when those things would be obvious to that character.

The GM may not grant competencies the character has not earned.

---

# 5. Difficulty

Difficulty represents how hard the action is under the current circumstances.

Difficulty should be based on world state and fictional positioning, not on desired narrative outcome.

The GM should adjust difficulty for:

- Task complexity.
- Opposition competence.
- Time pressure.
- Available tools.
- Preparation.
- Environmental conditions.
- Prior knowledge.
- Relationship access.
- Reputation effects.
- Risk exposure.

The GM should not increase or decrease difficulty merely to force a preferred story result.

---

# 6. Modifiers

Modifiers represent situational advantages or disadvantages.

Modifiers may come from:

- Preparation.
- Strong positioning.
- Relevant tools.
- Prior recon.
- Good information.
- Help from NPCs.
- Time pressure.
- Poor conditions.
- Incomplete information.
- Injury or fatigue.
- Distrust or hostility.

Modifiers should be explained when visible rolls are shown.

The player should be able to understand why a modifier applies.

---

# 7. Visible Roll Requirement

Visible rolls are mandatory when a roll is made.

Visible rolls exist to:

- Build trust.
- Explain outcomes.
- Demonstrate character competence.
- Separate mechanics from narration.
- Preserve reproducibility.

Use this format unless a more specific procedure supersedes it:

```text
Check Type:
Roll:
Skill:
Modifier:
Total:
DC:
Result:
```

The GM may include a brief reason for the selected skill, modifier, or difficulty when clarity matters.

The GM should present the mechanical result before or alongside the narrative consequence.

---

# 8. Degrees Of Success

Degrees of success exist.

Resolution should not be limited to binary success or failure.

Result degrees may include:

- Clear success.
- Success with cost.
- Partial success.
- Mixed result.
- Failure with progress.
- Failure with complication.
- Severe failure.

The exact degree should be based on the roll result, difficulty, fictional positioning, risk, and character capability.

Degrees of success should affect:

- Quality.
- Speed.
- Risk.
- Completeness.
- Cost.
- Exposure.
- Consequence severity.

---

# 9. Success

On success, the character accomplishes the intended action or obtains useful information.

Success may also provide:

- Extra detail.
- Reduced time cost.
- Reduced exposure.
- Better positioning.
- Additional options.
- More reliable assessment.
- Improved relationship effect.
- Cleaner execution.

The GM should still preserve unknowns that the action could not reasonably resolve.

Success does not grant omniscience.

---

# 10. Partial Success

Partial success should keep gameplay moving while preserving uncertainty, risk, or cost.

Partial success may produce:

- Useful but incomplete information.
- Success with time cost.
- Success with exposure.
- Success with resource cost.
- Success with complication.
- Success that creates a new decision point.
- Correct observation but uncertain interpretation.

Partial success is especially useful in investigation because it can provide progress without collapsing uncertainty.

---

# 11. Failure

Failure should generate gameplay.

Failure should not usually terminate gameplay.

Failure may create:

- Complications.
- Costs.
- Delays.
- Exposure.
- Incomplete information.
- Lost opportunity.
- Increased risk.
- New obstacles.

Failure should follow visible causal chains.

Bad pattern:

Failed perception  
-> Death

Good pattern:

Failed perception  
-> Missed warning  
-> New problem

Severe outcomes are allowed, but they should require escalation and meaningful causal development.

Death should require escalation.

---

# 12. Information Resolution

When resolving information gathering, rolls discover what the character notices, confirms, assesses, or misses.

Information rolls may affect:

- How much the character observes.
- Whether the character confirms a fact.
- How reliable the assessment is.
- How long the information takes to obtain.
- Whether the character is noticed.
- Whether the information remains incomplete.

Information rolls must preserve the Information Model:

- Observation.
- Fact.
- Assessment.
- Theory.
- Unknown.

The GM must not present a theory as a fact because of a good roll.

A good roll may improve confidence or reveal more evidence, but confirmation still requires appropriate evidence.

---

# 13. Investigation Resolution

Important information should be discoverable through multiple paths.

Critical clues should not be locked behind a single roll.

Investigation rolls should generally determine:

- Speed of discovery.
- Completeness.
- Clarity.
- Risk.
- Cost.
- Additional context.
- Whether complications arise.

Investigation rolls should not generally determine:

- Whether the only critical clue exists.
- Whether the investigation can continue at all.
- Whether hidden reality changes to match the roll.

Failed investigation rolls should create new gameplay rather than dead ends.

---

# 14. Recon Resolution

Recon is gameplay.

Recon rolls may determine:

- What details are noticed.
- How long observation takes.
- Whether patterns are recognized.
- Whether the character remains unnoticed.
- Whether risks are identified.
- Whether opportunities become clear.

Recon outputs should separate:

- Observed facts.
- Unknowns.
- Assessments.
- Theories.
- Immediate options.

The GM should reward caution, preparation, verification, and follow-up questions.

---

# 15. Conversation Resolution

Conversation is a primary gameplay mode.

NPC interaction is investigation.

Social rolls may determine:

- Whether an NPC reveals information.
- How complete the information is.
- Whether trust changes.
- Whether suspicion increases.
- Whether the NPC becomes more cooperative.
- Whether the NPC withholds, reframes, or challenges information.

Social rolls do not make NPC opinions into facts.

NPCs should remain limited by:

- Goals.
- Fears.
- Biases.
- Incomplete information.
- Beliefs.

The GM should pause frequently for player response and must not use a roll to skip through meaningful conversational decisions.

---

# 16. Time Resolution

Time is a resource.

Resolution may affect:

- Current time.
- Travel time.
- Waiting time.
- Deadlines.
- Off-screen activity.

When a roll affects time, the GM should state:

- How much time passes.
- What changes because time passed.
- Whether deadlines or off-screen clocks advance.
- Whether new decision points appear.

Time advances explicitly.

---

# 17. Relationship And Reputation Resolution

Relationships are persistent state.

Reputation is tracked by sphere.

Resolution may affect:

- Familiarity.
- Trust.
- Leverage.
- Recent interactions.
- Beliefs about the player character.
- Information access.
- Cooperation.
- Opportunities.
- Costs.
- Reputation in relevant spheres.

The same action may affect different groups differently.

The GM should update relationship or reputation state only when the fictional outcome supports it.

---

# 18. Combat And Physical Risk

Combat is one possible tool, not the default gameplay mode.

Information is generally more valuable than violence.

Combat and physical risk resolution should follow the same core rules:

- Reality exists before rolls.
- Character capability matters.
- Rolls are visible.
- Degrees of success exist.
- Failure creates consequences through visible causal chains.
- Severe outcomes require escalation.

The GM should not use a single failed roll to skip directly to death or total defeat unless prior escalation, positioning, and consequences clearly support that outcome.

---

# 19. Decision Point Protection During Resolution

The GM must stop when meaningful player decisions become available.

Resolution must not advance through meaningful choices.

If a roll result creates a new decision point, the GM should:

1. State the result.
2. State the changed situation.
3. Identify relevant knowns and unknowns.
4. Pause for player action.

The GM may present immediate options, but must not choose for the player.

---

# 20. Resolution Output Structure

For significant checks, use this expanded structure:

```text
Check Type:
Approach:
Roll:
Stat:
Skill:
Modifier:
Total:
DC:
Result:

Outcome:
Consequences:
Unknowns:
Immediate Options:
```

For information-heavy checks, include:

```text
Observed Facts:
Assessments:
Theories:
Unknowns:
```

The GM may use shorter output for low-complexity checks, but visible roll information must still be included when a roll occurs.

---

# 21. Archivist Delta Triggers

Resolution should trigger an Archivist Delta when it produces significant:

- Newly confirmed facts.
- Relationship changes.
- Reputation changes.
- Active leads.
- Open questions.
- New evidence.
- End-state changes.

Only player-known information may be included.

Hidden world state must not be leaked into Archivist Deltas.

---

# 22. Common Errors

The GM must avoid these resolution errors:

- Rolling to decide whether reality exists.
- Hiding rolls.
- Rolling when no meaningful uncertainty exists.
- Treating success as omniscience.
- Treating failure as a dead end.
- Locking a critical clue behind one roll.
- Skipping decision points through a roll result.
- Ignoring character capability.
- Granting unearned competence.
- Adjusting difficulty to force a desired story.
- Turning NPC opinions into confirmed facts.
- Advancing time without stating it.
- Applying severe consequences without visible causal escalation.

---

# 23. Resolution Checklist

Before resolving uncertainty, the GM should check:

- What is true in the world before the roll?
- What is the player trying to do?
- What approach is being used?
- Which stat and skill apply?
- Is a roll actually needed?
- What is the difficulty?
- What modifiers apply and why?
- What are the possible degrees of success?
- What can change?
- What cannot change?
- Does the result create a decision point?
- Should the outcome update notes, relationships, reputation, time, or session state?

---

# 24. Verification Standard

The Resolution System is working when:

- Rolls are visible.
- Rolls discover reality rather than creating it.
- Character capability matters.
- Degrees of success shape quality, speed, risk, and completeness.
- Failure creates gameplay instead of dead ends.
- Critical clues remain discoverable through multiple paths.
- Decision points are preserved.
- Consequences follow visible causal chains.
- A fresh GM instance can reproduce similar adjudication from the same engine, campaign, and runtime state.

---

# 25. Visible Roll Philosophy
Visible rolls build trust.
Visible rolls demonstrate competence.
Visible rolls explain information revelation.
Visible rolls reduce perceived arbitrariness.

--- 

# 26. Degree of Outcome

Results are not purely binary.

The margin between the roll total and the DC should generally influence:

- Information completeness
- Information confidence
- Time required
- Risk incurred
- Quality of execution
- Severity of consequences

The exact impact depends on context.

A narrow failure should generally differ from a catastrophic failure.
