# GAP ANALYSIS – SOLO RPG PROTOTYPE MISSION ENGINE

This analysis focuses on what was **present in gameplay** but either missing, underspecified, or insufficiently formalized in the extraction. These gaps are likely contributors to why a new Chapter 1 chat knew the setting content but failed to reproduce the gameplay feel.

---

# 1. DECISION POINT PROTECTION

## Missing Behavior

The extraction captured agency but not **decision point protection**.

In the PM, the GM repeatedly protected meaningful decisions from being invalidated by hidden information, surprise death, or arbitrary state changes.

## Why It Matters

This is one of the biggest reasons the PM felt fair.

The player could make decisions confidently because:

- Hidden information existed.
    
- Unknowns existed.
    
- But decisions were rarely rendered meaningless by unseen gotchas.
    

The player felt:

> "I can make plans."

instead of:

> "The GM will invalidate whatever I choose."

## Example From Play

Discussion around:

- being shot from behind
    
- failed perception
    
- death
    

The agreed model became:

Failure escalates danger.

Failure does not skip directly to consequences several causal layers away.

Bad:

> Failed perception -> dead.

Good:

> Failed perception -> guard closes distance -> new problem.

## Recommended Engine Rule

Decision-Relevance Rule:

Player decisions should fail through visible causal chains.

The GM should avoid hidden-state outcomes that bypass meaningful player response opportunities.

---

# 2. CONVERSATION AS PRIMARY GAMEPLAY

## Missing Behavior

The extraction treats NPC interaction as a subsystem.

In actual play it was one of the primary gameplay loops.

## Why It Matters

A large portion of PM enjoyment came from:

- reading people
    
- probing motivations
    
- testing hypotheses
    
- social maneuvering
    

The game often advanced more through conversation than physical action.

## Example From Play

Linda conversation.

The player:

- gathered intel
    
- tested reactions
    
- paid for information
    
- evaluated credibility
    

without combat, skill challenges, or infiltration.

## Recommended Engine Rule

Conversation is a first-class gameplay mode.

NPC interactions should be treated as investigative scenes, not exposition delivery mechanisms.

---

# 3. RECONNAISSANCE IS THE PRIMARY GAME LOOP

## Missing Behavior

Recon was mentioned but not elevated to a central gameplay structure.

## Why It Matters

PM repeatedly followed:

Observe → Learn → Refine Understanding → Act

not

Act → Roll → Outcome

This is fundamentally different from most RPG structures.

## Example From Play

Diner.

Footage.

Helix drive-by.

Client analysis.

Each step gathered information before committing to action.

## Recommended Engine Rule

Default mission flow should favor reconnaissance and information gathering before action phases.

The GM should actively support reconnaissance opportunities.

---

# 4. GM SHOULD REWARD CAUTIOUS THINKING

## Missing Behavior

The extraction captured facts-first but not the reward structure.

## Why It Matters

The player repeatedly received valuable information because they were cautious and observant.

The system incentivized professionalism.

## Example From Play

Asking follow-up questions.

Reviewing timelines.

Checking assumptions.

Reconstructing events.

These were productive activities.

## Recommended Engine Rule

Professional caution should produce meaningful advantages.

The GM should avoid making careful play feel wasted.

---

# 5. CHARACTER THOUGHTS AS A SYSTEM

## Missing Behavior

Mentioned but underspecified.

## Why It Matters

This became one of the strongest tools for preserving character identity.

The GM occasionally distinguished:

Jimmy thought.

Nick thought.

## Example From Play

Several moments:

> "A thought occurs to Nick..."

These were not facts.

Not commands.

Not narration.

They were character-perspective guidance.

## Recommended Engine Rule

The GM may provide Character Insight prompts.

These should:

- reflect character experience
    
- not override player decisions
    
- not reveal hidden truths
    

---

# 6. INFORMATION PRESENTATION STRUCTURE

## Missing Behavior

The extraction identifies information layers but not presentation format.

## Why It Matters

The PM consistently separated:

Observation.

Assessment.

Character conclusion.

World fact.

This dramatically improved clarity.

## Example From Play

Repeated structure:

Observation Check

Roll

Result

Assessment

Character Conclusion

## Recommended Engine Rule

The GM should separate:

- sensory facts
    
- inferred facts
    
- character assessments
    
- GM certainty
    

into distinct sections.

---

# 7. NPC EXPOSITION LIMITS

## Missing Behavior

Not adequately captured.

## Why It Matters

NPCs rarely dumped information.

Instead:

- answered questions
    
- revealed limited perspectives
    
- challenged assumptions
    

## Example From Play

Marcus.

He rarely solved problems.

He reframed them.

## Recommended Engine Rule

NPCs should provide perspective more often than solutions.

NPCs should know less than the GM.

---

# 8. SOUNDING BOARD NPCS

## Missing Behavior

Mentioned but not formalized.

## Why It Matters

Marcus became critical gameplay infrastructure.

Not because of content.

Because of function.

## Example From Play

Marcus repeatedly:

- challenged assumptions
    
- identified blind spots
    
- proposed alternate interpretations
    

without solving mysteries.

## Recommended Engine Rule

Long campaigns should include trusted NPCs whose purpose is perspective, not problem-solving.

---

# 9. SCENE ADVANCEMENT CRITERIA

## Missing Behavior

Major omission.

## Why It Matters

The PM advanced scenes naturally.

The Chapter 1 testing likely lost this.

## Example From Play

Warehouse scene ended because:

- no new facts remained
    
- next leads existed elsewhere
    

not because:

- timer expired
    
- dialogue quota reached
    

## Recommended Engine Rule

Advance scenes when information yield diminishes and stronger opportunities exist elsewhere.

---

# 10. TIME AS A RESOURCE

## Missing Behavior

Tracked but not formalized.

## Why It Matters

Time was one of the most important resources in the PM.

## Example From Play

Questions repeatedly included:

- How long?
    
- Do I have time?
    
- Can I do both?
    

## Recommended Engine Rule

The GM should consistently surface meaningful time costs.

---

# 11. PLAYER NOTE GENERATION

## Missing Behavior

Almost completely missing.

## Why It Matters

The PM accumulated:

- theories
    
- leads
    
- evidence
    
- contacts
    

No formal structure existed.

## Recommended Engine Rule

At major transitions generate:

Known Facts

Open Questions

Active Leads

Potential Theories

This should be explicit.

---

# 12. SESSION LOGGING

## Missing Behavior

Underspecified.

## Why It Matters

A new chat cannot reproduce PM feel without state continuity.

## Recommended Engine Rule

Session summaries should track:

- current time
    
- conditions
    
- inventory
    
- contacts
    
- active investigations
    
- known facts
    
- unresolved questions
    

---

# 13. UNCERTAINTY PRESENTATION

## Missing Behavior

Critical omission.

## Why It Matters

One of the strongest PM traits was uncertainty honesty.

The GM often said:

> Possible.

> Plausible.

> Unknown.

> You don't know.

instead of inventing certainty.

## Recommended Engine Rule

The GM should explicitly indicate confidence levels.

Facts and uncertainty must remain distinct.

---

# 14. CHARACTER SHEET UTILIZATION

## Missing Behavior

Huge gap.

## Why It Matters

The PM used skills constantly.

But the sheet was never surfaced.

The player eventually lost connection to character capability.

## Example From Play

The Jimmy vs Nick discussion.

## Recommended Engine Rule

The sheet should be visible and referenced regularly.

The GM should explain when character expertise influences interpretation.

---

# 15. RELATIONSHIP STATE TRACKING

## Missing Behavior

Underspecified.

## Why It Matters

Relationships drove much of gameplay.

Yet no formal structure existed.

## Example From Play

Marcus.

Linda.

Client.

Trust evolved through play.

## Recommended Engine Rule

Track:

- familiarity
    
- trust
    
- leverage
    
- reputation
    

as persistent state.

---

# 16. WORLD STATE VS STORY STATE

## Missing Behavior

Possibly the most important omission.

## Why It Matters

The PM felt alive because the GM modeled a world.

Chapter 1 likely reverted to modeling a story.

## Example From Play

Patrol example.

Hidden movement.

Off-screen actors.

Persistent reality.

## Recommended Engine Rule

The GM should model:

World State first.

Story second.

Narrative emerges from state changes.

---

# 17. PROFESSIONAL COMPETENCE FILTER

## Missing Behavior

Not documented.

## Why It Matters

The PM repeatedly prevented player self-sabotage through expertise.

## Example From Play

USB malware discussion.

The GM effectively said:

> Your character would know better.

without removing agency.

## Recommended Engine Rule

When expertise applies, the GM may surface likely professional considerations the character would naturally think of.

---

# 18. CHAPTER 1 FAILURE INDICATOR

## Likely Missing From Engine

The new DM knew:

- setting
    
- lore
    
- campaign content
    

But did not know:

- how to pace scenes
    
- how to challenge assumptions
    
- how to structure investigations
    
- how to separate fact/theory
    
- how to use NPCs as sounding boards
    
- how to present uncertainty
    
- how to use character expertise
    
- how to advance scenes
    
- how to maintain decision-point protection
    

These are not content problems.

They are engine problems.

---

# Highest Priority Missing Rules

If only five additional rules were added to the engine documentation, I would choose:

1. Decision Point Protection
    
2. Conversation as Primary Gameplay
    
3. Reconnaissance as Core Gameplay Loop
    
4. World State First, Story Second
    
5. Explicit Fact → Observation → Assessment → Theory Presentation Structure
    

Those five behaviors account for a surprisingly large percentage of what made the Prototype Mission feel distinct from a typical AI-run RPG.
