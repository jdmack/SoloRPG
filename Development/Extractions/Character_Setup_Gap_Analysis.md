# Gap Analysis: Solo RPG Engine Extraction

## 1. Decision Point Protection

### Missing Behavior

The extraction said the GM should not override player decisions, but it did not formalize **decision point protection**.

### Why It Matters

This is probably one of the biggest reasons Chapter 1 felt different. The GM must recognize when a meaningful decision is available and stop advancing.

### Example From Play

The desired opening was: montage until Marcus offers a job, then transition into play. The risk is that the GM continues narrating Nick accepting, investigating, reacting, or emotionally processing without player input.

### Recommended Engine Rule

When a scene reaches a meaningful choice, the GM must stop and ask for the player’s action. The GM may frame options but must not choose. Meaningful choices include accepting jobs, choosing investigative approach, confronting NPCs, escalating violence, revealing information, spending money/resources, trusting someone, or advancing time.

---

## 2. Interactive Conversation Flow

### Missing Behavior

The extraction did not capture that gameplay should be **turn-by-turn conversational**, not prose-forward narration.

### Why It Matters

The PM worked because the player could interrupt, question, investigate, ask clarifying questions, and shape the approach. A new DM chat may write long novel-like passages and skip the playable parts.

### Example From Play

The player wanted to handle recon, questions, NPC reads, and note-taking interactively instead of being handed a summary.

### Recommended Engine Rule

The GM should keep responses playable. Describe the situation, present actionable information, then pause. Avoid resolving multiple steps of a plan in one response unless the player explicitly asks for a montage or summary.

---

## 3. Scene Advancement Rules

### Missing Behavior

The extraction mentioned skipping uninteresting time but did not define **when scenes advance**.

### Why It Matters

Without scene advancement rules, the GM may rush past investigations or stall in trivial detail.

### Example From Play

The Chapter 1 opening should skip months of failed job hunting, but once Marcus offers a job, play should slow down.

### Recommended Engine Rule

Use three pacing modes:

1. **Montage** for low-agency time.
    
2. **Scene play** for dialogue, decisions, danger, discovery, or relationship movement.
    
3. **Zoomed recon mode** for surveillance/investigation where small observations matter.
    

The GM should explicitly signal pacing shifts.

---

## 4. Reconnaissance as Primary Gameplay

### Missing Behavior

The extraction said recon matters, but did not define recon as an actual play loop.

### Why It Matters

The PM felt good because recon was not just flavor. It generated facts, options, risk assessment, and tactical leverage.

### Example From Play

The player enjoyed scouting Helix, observing patterns, assessing entrances/exits, and forming plans from details.

### Recommended Engine Rule

Recon scenes should produce:

- Observable facts
    
- Environmental layout
    
- People present
    
- Routines/patterns
    
- Security posture
    
- Unknowns
    
- Risks
    
- Potential approaches
    

Recon should never be handwaved when the player chooses it as their main action.

---

## 5. Visible Roll Philosophy

### Missing Behavior

The extraction identified this as missing but did not explain the likely intended behavior.

### Why It Matters

Visible rolls create trust. They show that uncertainty is being adjudicated rather than narrated arbitrarily.

### Example From Play

The PM apparently used visible rolls enough that later prompts specifically called out “visible roll philosophy.”

### Recommended Engine Rule

When outcome uncertainty matters, the GM should show the roll, modifier/reasoning, difficulty, and result. Do not roll for obvious success, obvious failure, or purely informational description.

---

## 6. Character Sheet Usage

### Missing Behavior

The extraction captured qualitative skills but not how the GM should actively use the character sheet.

### Why It Matters

Chapter 1 Nick should feel different from PM Nick. Without active sheet usage, the GM may accidentally give early Nick advanced fixer/tradecraft abilities.

### Example From Play

Chapter 1 Nick is strong at combat/recon but weak at networking, informants, cover stories, and criminal ecosystem navigation.

### Recommended Engine Rule

Before resolving an action, the GM should check the character’s established competencies. Strong skills reduce difficulty or increase information quality. Weak skills increase risk, cost, or complication. The GM must not grant future-developed skills early unless earned in play.

---

## 7. Information Presentation Format

### Missing Behavior

The extraction said facts and conclusions should be separate, but did not specify a repeatable output format.

### Why It Matters

Investigation gameplay needs clean information. If the GM mixes fact, tone, suspicion, and conclusion together, the player cannot reason clearly.

### Example From Play

The user explicitly wanted Helix recon details saved in a way that was easy to view and update.

### Recommended Engine Rule

For investigation/recon outputs, use structured sections when useful:

- **Observed Facts**
    
- **Notable Details**
    
- **Unclear / Unknown**
    
- **Possible Interpretations**
    
- **Immediate Options**
    

The GM should avoid presenting theories as truth.

---

## 8. Fact vs Observation vs Assessment vs Theory

### Missing Behavior

This distinction was mentioned but not formalized.

### Why It Matters

This is central to the kind of play the user enjoys. The player wants grounded reasoning, not mystery smoke.

### Example From Play

A vehicle at a location is an observation. “They are conducting trafficking” is a theory unless confirmed.

### Recommended Engine Rule

Use four categories:

- **Observation:** What Nick directly perceives.
    
- **Fact:** Verified information.
    
- **Assessment:** Nick’s professional read based on experience.
    
- **Theory:** A possible explanation requiring confirmation.
    

The GM must label uncertainty clearly.

## 9. Player Note Generation

### Missing Behavior

The extraction discussed repository structure but not when/how the GM generates player-facing notes.

### Why It Matters

The player hates taking notes but needs usable intelligence files.

### Example From Play

The whole VS Code/Codex Archivist workflow emerged because chat scrolling was unmanageable.

### Recommended Engine Rule

After any significant recon, conversation, discovery, or session, the GM should offer or generate an **Archivist Delta** containing only player-known information, suitable for Intelligence files.

## 10. Session Logging Procedures

### Missing Behavior

The extraction said session summaries should exist but did not define what they contain.

### Why It Matters

Long-running campaigns need session continuity without rereading full chats.

### Example From Play

The repo has `Sessions/`, but no formal session note schema was finalized.

### Recommended Engine Rule

At session end, generate:

- Session title/date
    
- Major events
    
- Decisions made
    
- NPCs encountered
    
- New facts learned
    
- Open leads
    
- Changes to relationships/reputation
    
- Resources gained/lost
    
- Archivist Delta

## 11. GM Interruption Rules

### Missing Behavior

The extraction did not capture when the GM should interrupt the player.

### Why It Matters

A grounded game needs the GM to warn when the player is about to act on a false assumption, missing obvious character knowledge, or skipping an important risk.

### Example From Play

The user asked the assistant to “hold my hand” when managing prompts and system extraction. In gameplay, similar guidance is needed without taking control.

### Recommended Engine Rule

The GM may interrupt before action resolution when:

- The player misunderstands previously established facts.
    
- The character would obviously notice a risk.
    
- The action would cause major irreversible consequences.
    
- The player is skipping a clear decision point.
    

The GM should frame it as clarification, not correction.

---

## 12. NPC Exposition Limits

### Missing Behavior

NPC behavior was discussed, but not NPC exposition limits.

### Why It Matters

NPCs should not dump plot. Investigation should remain interactive.

### Example From Play

Marcus should not simply explain the whole first case. He should provide what he knows, what he suspects, and what he needs.

### Recommended Engine Rule

NPCs should reveal information based on what they know, want, fear, and are willing to say. They should be incomplete, biased, or cautious without being artificially obscure.

## 13. Time Advancement Procedures

### Missing Behavior

Time compression was mentioned, but no procedure was defined for clocks, surveillance duration, travel, waiting, or consequences over time.

### Why It Matters

Fixer/investigation play depends on timing: stakeouts, routines, deadlines, police response, target movement.

### Example From Play

The desired opening involves six months to one year passing in montage, then slowing into normal play.

### Recommended Engine Rule

The GM should track time in meaningful units:

- Immediate scene
    
- Hours
    
- Day/night
    
- Days
    
- Weeks/months montage
    

When the player advances time, the GM should state what changes, what remains unknown, and whether opportunities or risks evolve.

## 14. Relationship Tracking

### Missing Behavior

The extraction recognized relationships as important but did not define tracking.

### Why It Matters

The campaign depends on slowly built support networks.

### Example From Play

Marcus begins reluctant for reputational reasons, not personal hostility. That nuance needs tracking.

### Recommended Engine Rule

Track each important relationship with:

- Disposition
    
- Trust level
    
- Leverage owed/held
    
- Recent interactions
    
- Known boundaries
    
- Reputation effects
    
- What they believe about the PC

## 15. Trust and Reputation Systems

### Missing Behavior

Reputation was identified as important but not systematized.

### Why It Matters

Fixer gameplay runs on reputation. Jobs, prices, risks, and allies depend on what people believe.

### Example From Play

Nick’s dishonorable discharge is a liability in legitimate society but may later become credibility in criminal circles.

### Recommended Engine Rule

Track reputation by sphere, not globally. Example spheres:

- Legitimate society
    
- Criminal street level
    
- Professional underworld
    
- Law enforcement
    
- Former military/veterans
    
- Local neighborhood
    

Each sphere can have different beliefs and consequences.

## 16. Presenting Uncertainty

### Missing Behavior

The extraction said uncertainty should be honest, but not how to phrase it.

### Why It Matters

The player prefers facts over random twists. The GM must not fake certainty.

### Example From Play

During recon, “the guard looks nervous” is not the same as “the guard is hiding something.”

### Recommended Engine Rule

Use uncertainty language:

- “You see…”
    
- “You can confirm…”
    
- “Your read is…”
    
- “A reasonable possibility is…”
    
- “You do not yet know…”
    

Never present an inference as confirmed truth unless it is confirmed.

---

## 17. Partial Success Handling

### Missing Behavior

The extraction said failure creates complications, but did not define partial success.

### Why It Matters

Partial success is ideal for investigations because it keeps momentum while preserving risk.

### Example From Play

A recon roll might reveal the building layout but miss the hidden camera, or identify a routine but attract mild suspicion.

### Recommended Engine Rule

Partial success should usually provide useful information plus one cost:

- Time cost
    
- Increased suspicion
    
- Incomplete information
    
- Resource expense
    
- Exposure to danger
    
- Future complication
    

---

## 18. Clue Discovery Procedure

### Missing Behavior

The extraction said clues must exist but not how they are discovered.

### Why It Matters

Mystery gameplay fails if essential clues are hidden behind single rolls.

### Example From Play

The PM felt good because investigation produced usable detail rather than binary “you find the clue / you don’t.”

### Recommended Engine Rule

Critical clues should not be locked behind one roll. Rolls affect clarity, speed, cost, risk, or extra context. Multiple routes should exist to important information.

## 19. Scene Closure Procedure

### Missing Behavior

No procedure for ending a scene.

### Why It Matters

Without closure, important facts and choices may blur together.

### Example From Play

After recon or NPC meetings, the player needs a clean summary of what changed.

### Recommended Engine Rule

At the end of significant scenes, provide:

- What happened
    
- What Nick learned
    
- What remains unresolved
    
- Immediate options
    
- Suggested Archivist Delta if appropriate

## 20. New DM Startup Requirements

### Missing Behavior

The extraction said a new DM needs style and system docs but did not define a startup checklist.

### Why It Matters

Chapter 1 failure proves a new chat can know content but not run the engine.

### Example From Play

The Chapter 1 chat had campaign background but lacked the PM gameplay procedures.

### Recommended Engine Rule

A new DM chat must receive:

- Current character state
    
- Current intelligence state
    
- System design rules
    
- GM principles
    
- Resolution/roll rules
    
- Information presentation rules
    
- Decision point protection rule
    
- Current scene start
    
- Explicit instruction to pause at decision points
    

---

# Critical Overall Gap

The previous extraction captured **philosophy** better than **procedure**.

It identified that the campaign should be grounded, investigative, and relationship-driven, but did not fully document the operational behaviors that make that happen turn by turn.

The most important missing engine rules are:

1. Stop at decision points.
    
2. Use structured investigation outputs.
    
3. Separate observations, facts, assessments, and theories.
    
4. Use visible rolls when uncertainty matters.
    
5. Treat recon as a primary gameplay loop.
    
6. Generate player-known Archivist Deltas.
    
7. Track relationships and reputation by sphere.
    
8. Advance time deliberately.
    
9. Prevent NPC exposition dumps.
    
10. Start new chats with system rules, not just campaign canon.
