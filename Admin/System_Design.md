# System Design

## Campaign Type

The Rise of a Fixer is a long-running solo RPG focused on investigation, reputation, relationships, criminal ecosystems, and problem solving.

The campaign is grounded and realistic in tone.

The campaign is not:

* A superhero story
* A save-the-world story
* A global conspiracy story
* A spy thriller
* A power fantasy

## Core Philosophy

The world exists independently of the player.

NPCs have goals, motivations, fears, resources, and constraints.

Events occur because of NPC decisions and world conditions, not because the story requires them.

## Information Model

Information is divided into:

### Canon

Objective reality.

Stored under:

Canon/

### Intelligence

Information known by Nick Easton and/or the player.

Stored under:

Intelligence/

Canon information must never be leaked into Intelligence until discovered during play.

## Investigations

Investigations are evidence-driven.

Conclusions should emerge from facts.

The GM should avoid arbitrary reveals.

The player should be rewarded for:

* Surveillance
* Interviews
* Reconnaissance
* Research
* Analysis
* Building relationships

## Violence

Violence is a tool.

Violence has consequences.

Violence can solve problems but can also create new problems.

The campaign should not assume violence is the preferred solution.

## Competence

Nick is competent.

Nick is not superhuman.

Success should come primarily from:

* Preparation
* Observation
* Relationships
* Planning
* Skill

rather than plot armor.

## Failure

Failure should create complications.

Failure should rarely create dead ends.

When possible:

Failure moves the story sideways rather than stopping it.

## Character Growth

Chapter 1 Nick begins primarily as:

* Reconnaissance specialist
* Observer
* Tracker
* Shooter

Over time he develops:

* Investigative skills
* Tradecraft
* Informant handling
* Criminal networking
* Negotiation
* Business relationships

The rise from operator to fixer is a major campaign theme.

## Visual Asset Policy

Visual assets are divided into three categories.

### Level 1 - Reference Assets

Examples:

- Vehicles
- Weapons
- Tools
- Equipment
- Clothing
- Real-world buildings

Preferred Method:

Image search and real-world reference images.

Goal:

Fast visual grounding and repository continuity.

Storage:

Images/Assets/

Examples:

- Toyota_Hilux_1995.png
- Glock_19.png
- Detroit_Duplex.png

### Level 2 - Persistent Campaign Assets

Examples:

- Recurring NPCs
- Recurring locations
- Safehouses
- Businesses
- Organization headquarters

Preferred Method:

Image generation.

Goal:

Create a consistent visual identity for campaign-specific people and places.

Storage:

Images/Characters/
Images/Locations/

### Level 3 - Cinematic Assets

Examples:

- Chapter covers
- Major story moments
- Flashbacks
- Significant confrontations
- Important discoveries

Preferred Method:

Image generation.

Goal:

Capture important moments and reinforce campaign tone.

Storage:

Images/Scenes/

### General Principle

Use image search when visual accuracy and speed are more important than uniqueness.

Use image generation when creating campaign-specific people, places, or iconic moments.

Avoid generating images for every scene.

Generate images when the asset is expected to remain relevant beyond the current scene or is important enough to justify a permanent visual reference.
